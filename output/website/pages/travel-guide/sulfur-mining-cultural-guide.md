# Ijen Sulfur Mining — History, Culture & Visitor Etiquette

The sulfur miners of Kawah Ijen are not a tourist attraction. They are workers — carrying heavy loads of broken sulfur up a steep crater path in direct exposure to toxic gases. They share the trail with you. Understanding their work, their history, and the etiquette of sharing their space is part of being a responsible visitor.

## Overview

Sulfur mining at Ijen is one of the last manual sulfur extraction operations in the world. An estimated 200–300 miners currently work the crater, employed by **PT Candi Ngrimbi**, which holds the mining concession. The sulfur is used in industrial processes — sugar bleaching, rubber vulcanization, fertilizer production.

The miners descend to the crater floor where sulfuric gases vent from active fumaroles. They break solidified sulfur from the deposits, load it into bamboo baskets balanced on shoulder poles, and carry it up the steep crater path to the weighing station at Paltuding. Twice daily. In darkness for the first trip.

This is not heritage tourism in the museum sense. The mining is active, ongoing, and physically punishing. Visitors walking the same trail witness a living industry — not a reconstruction.

## History

Sulfur mining at Ijen dates to the **early 1900s under Dutch colonial exploitation**. The operation has continued largely unchanged for over a century. The extraction method — manual breaking, hand-carrying, gravity as the only transport — is essentially the same as it was during the colonial period.

The mining concession passed through various hands after Indonesian independence. PT Candi Ngrimbi currently operates the concession. The workforce has remained local — drawn from villages around the Ijen plateau, primarily in Banyuwangi and Bondowoso regencies.

The persistence of manual methods in a modern industrial context makes Ijen unusual. Mechanized extraction would be more efficient, but the terrain (steep crater walls, acidic environment, gas exposure) and economics (low sulfur prices, available labor force) have kept the manual system in place.

## Working Conditions

The physical reality of Ijen sulfur mining:

- **Loads**: 70–90 kg per trip, carried in bamboo baskets on shoulder poles up the ~700 m crater ascent and then ~3 km to the Paltuding weighing station
- **Frequency**: Two trips per day (first trip begins before dawn)
- **Income**: Approximately IDR 50,000–100,000 per load (roughly USD 3–6) — one of the most physically demanding low-wage jobs in Indonesia
- **Gas exposure**: Miners work in direct contact with SO₂ and HCl gases. Most work without gas masks.
- **Health impact**: Chronic respiratory problems, skin burns, and dental erosion are common. Life expectancy for long-term miners is significantly reduced.
- **Tenure**: Many miners have worked the crater for 20+ years. It is generational work in some families.

The contrast with tourists is visible on the trail. Visitors wear gas masks (provided by JVTO as standard inclusions). Miners carry loads that exceed most visitors' body weight, in the same gas environment, without respiratory protection.

## Sharing the Trail

The trail from Paltuding to the crater rim is shared between tourists and working miners. This is their workplace — visitors are guests on it.

**Miners carrying loads always have right of way.**

The path is narrow in sections. When a miner approaches carrying a loaded basket, step aside and let them pass. They cannot easily stop or maneuver with 70–90 kg on their shoulders. Blocking the trail — even unintentionally — forces them to expend energy they cannot afford.

JVTO guides brief this before departure. It is the single most important etiquette point at Ijen.

## Photography & Interaction

### Photography

- **Ask before photographing miners.** A nod or gesture requesting permission is sufficient. Most will agree.
- **Do not use flash** — particularly not in their faces during the pre-dawn descent.
- **Do not photograph miners in distress** — if someone is visibly struggling with a load or coughing from gas exposure, that is not a photo opportunity.
- **Scenic shots of the trail, crater, and mining operation from a respectful distance** are generally fine.

### Souvenir Purchasing

Some miners sell small sulfur souvenirs — shaped figures (turtles, flowers, animals) and raw sulfur pieces — at the crater rim or along the trail. This is **supplemental income** beyond their mining wages.

- **Buy from dedicated souvenir sellers** — miners who have stopped to sell, not those carrying working loads
- **Do not attempt to buy from a miner mid-carry** — those sulfur blocks are destined for the weighing station and represent their wage for that trip
- **Haggling is fine** for souvenirs, but context matters — you are negotiating with someone who earns IDR 50,000–100,000 per 70 kg carry

### Tips

Miners may ask for tips. This is common and not aggressive. Respond as you see fit. There is no obligation, and no pressure is applied through JVTO guides.

## FAQ

**Q: Is it safe to interact with the miners?**
Yes. Miners are accustomed to tourists on the trail. Most are friendly and will pose for photos if asked. The key is timing — interact when they are resting or selling souvenirs, not when carrying loads.

**Q: Should I tip the miners?**
There is no obligation. Some visitors do, some do not. If a miner poses for a photo or assists you on the trail, a tip is a reasonable gesture. JVTO guides do not pressure guests either way.

**Q: Can I try carrying a sulfur load?**
Some miners offer to let tourists try lifting their baskets for a photo. This is between you and the miner. Be aware that 70–90 kg is significantly heavier than it looks, and the shoulder poles distribute weight differently than a backpack.
