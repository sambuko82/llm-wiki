## Post 2 — Medical Screening Coordination at Ijen (C4) — Both

**Caption (Instagram):**
Before the Ijen night hike, your vitals get checked.

Ijen access rules can require a recent local health certificate — that's a BBKSDA regulation, not something JVTO invented. When current access rules require it, JVTO coordinates the clinic workflow with Dr. Ahmad Irwandanu (SIP-licensed, Kemenkes-verifiable).

Blood pressure. Oxygen saturation. Heart rate. Logged digitally. A QR-verified surat sehat is issued — and scanned by BBKSDA staff at the crater access gate. No valid QR code, no entry.

That gate exists for a reason: you're hiking at 2,386 m, breathing sulfur gas. The check happens at your hotel before you leave.

Dr. Irwandanu's license is publicly checkable at satusehat.kemkes.go.id before you book.

**Caption:**
The Ijen blue fire is one of the rarest natural phenomena on earth. It's not a light show — it's the combustion of sulfuric gas escaping from underground vents at the crater floor, visible most clearly in the two hours before dawn.

**Caption:**
Madakaripura is not a typical waterfall. Local tradition holds the canyon as the final meditation site of Gajah Mada — the 14th-century prime minister of the Majapahit Empire. The narrow canyon walls rise 100 m on all sides. At the far end, water drops in a near-complete circle.

The access requires wading. You will get wet. That's built into the JVTO itinerary — ponchos and guidance on footwear are part of the briefing.

Madakaripura appears in JVTO's Bromo + Madakaripura packages. Your guide leads the canyon at your pace, private group only.
